01 · Core Philosophy
Two fundamentally different emotional strategies
The existing App Design System uses Trust Architecture — warm terracotta on light surfaces to convey approachability and safety in a high-stakes purchase. The Marketing Web Design System uses Dark Intelligence — cyan on deep navy to project technological sophistication and data-driven authority. These are not just different colors; they are different emotional contracts with the user.

Trust Architecture Issues: White-on-terracotta buttons (3.7:1) technically fail WCAG AA for normal text — but pass for large text, and buttons use 16px SemiBold which qualifies as "large." The real problem is #A89E8E tertiary text at 2.6:1 — this fails even for large text. Recommendation: darken tertiary to at least #8A8070 (~3.5:1) or #706858 (~5:1 for AA normal).

05 · Recommendations
Verdict & Thought Process
After analyzing both systems across color psychology, WCAG compliance, typography, and five key screens, here is my detailed recommendation.

What Works

Trust Architecture: Emotional Fit

For a mobile app used daily during a stressful, months-long home purchase, warm colors reduce cognitive load and anxiety. The cream-on-white surfaces feel inviting, like a personal assistant's notebook rather than a dashboard. This is the right emotional register for the product's core use case.

Dark Intelligence: First Impressions

For a marketing website where you have seconds to establish credibility, the dark cinematic approach works brilliantly. It positions ALON as a premium AI product, not another real estate portal. The cyan-on-navy hero sections feel authoritative and data-driven.

Dark Intelligence: CTA Accessibility

Navy text on cyan buttons (10.5:1) dramatically outperforms white on terracotta (3.7:1). If the app must use terracotta CTAs, consider switching button text to navy (#0D1F4A) for better contrast, or darken terracotta to #B84920.

Both: Typography Foundation

DM Serif Display + DM Sans is an excellent pairing in both systems. The web addition of Space Mono for section labels adds a useful hierarchy layer that could benefit the app too (for stage labels, section headers).

What Doesn't Work

Dark Intelligence for Mobile App

A full-dark mobile app for daily use during home buying would cause eye fatigue, feel clinical rather than supportive, and clash with the "personal assistant" metaphor. Dark mode works for entertainment (Netflix) and analytics (Bloomberg), but not for a companion app you trust with your biggest purchase. Dark is wrong for the app.

Trust Architecture for Marketing Web

Warm cream surfaces on a marketing website would look like a lifestyle blog, not an AI-powered product. In the Indian proptech space where competitors use aggressive blues and greens, the warm palette would appear soft and uncommanding. Warm is wrong for the website.

Both: Tertiary Text Contrast

Trust Architecture's #A89E8E (2.6:1) and Dark Intelligence's #4E6180 (3.1:1) both fail WCAG AA for normal text. Both need remediation — TA should use #706858 minimum, DI should use #6B7D99 minimum for tertiary text.

Adopting DI Colors into the App

Cyan (#00D4C8) as the app's primary accent would feel cold and transactional. It lacks the warmth needed for trust-building moments like activation, onboarding, and preference confirmation. Cyan says "technology"; terracotta says "I've got you."

Final Recommendation: Dual System with Shared DNA

These are not competing design systems. They serve different touchpoints in the same funnel: the website converts, the app retains. The right answer is not "pick one" — it's "use both, with intentional bridges."

  • Keep Trust Architecture for the mobile app. Warm terracotta + cream surfaces + navy text is the right emotional contract for a daily-use companion during a high-stakes journey. Fix the tertiary text contrast (#A89E8E to #706858) and consider darkening CTA terracotta slightly (#C45525) for better white-text contrast.
  • Keep Dark Intelligence for the marketing website. Cyan + deep navy + cinematic sections sell the AI-powered vision. Space Mono section labels add tech credibility. The 10.5:1 CTA contrast is excellent. Fix tertiary (#4E6180 to #6B7D99).
  • Bridge the two systems with shared elements: Same DM Serif Display + DM Sans typeface pairing. Same status colors (green/amber/red). Same icon library (Lucide). Same component structure (cards, pills, sheets). Same ALON eye mark. The transition from website to app should feel like walking from a sleek showroom into a warm consultation room — different mood, same brand.
  • Adopt Space Mono from DI into the app for stage labels and section eyebrows — it adds useful hierarchy without changing the emotional register.
  • Adopt the "Cyan Highlight Rule" from DI for web only. In the app, rely on font weight for emphasis as currently specified — cyan highlight text would feel foreign in the warm app context.
  • Consider a "dark mode" toggle in the app that uses DI colors — but only as an option, never the default. Some users prefer dark interfaces, and having a dark mode that matches the website creates brand coherence.

Thought Process Summary

Decision Factor Reasoning
Context > Aesthetics Both palettes are well-designed. The question is not "which looks better" but "which serves the user's emotional state at that touchpoint." A nervous first-time buyer needs warmth in-app. A skeptical website visitor needs authority in the hero.
Daily use vs First impression Dark interfaces win at "wow" but lose at "daily comfort." Studies show dark UIs increase perceived sophistication but decrease perceived warmth. For an app used 50+ times over months, warmth wins. For a 30-second landing page, wow wins.
Indian market positioning Indian proptech competitors (99acres, MagicBricks, NoBroker) all use light themes with blue/green accents. ALON's terracotta is already distinctive in the app space. The dark marketing site is even more distinctive — no competitor uses cinematic dark heroes.
WCAG as a tiebreaker Where both designs are emotionally valid, accessibility should decide. DI's CTA contrast (10.5:1) is objectively better than TA's (3.7:1). This doesn't mean switch to cyan app-wide, but it does mean fix TA's button contrast by darkening terracotta or using navy button text.
Brand coherence A dual system works only if the two halves share DNA. Same typefaces, same icon library, same component patterns, same ALON eye mark, same status colors. The differences should be limited to: background treatment, accent color, and surface warmth. Everything else stays unified.
